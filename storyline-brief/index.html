§ 00 · how to use

How to read this brief.

A working document, not a script. Dawon adapts every episode to feel like her — the brief sets the boundary, not the words.

Every episode has the same five blocks

BlockWhat it is
Emotional beatWhat the audience feels — the one-paragraph north star for the episode
Reel structure30–90 second vertical video, beat-by-beat with timestamps
Carousel structure15–20 static slides expanding the same story
Caption templateThe Instagram caption draft, with hashtags + helpline links where relevant
Safety / strategy notesWhat to watch for, what never appears on camera, what's mandatory

How the two formats relate

StepWhat happens
1. Reel films firstShort, hooky, emotionally tight. Tests how the beat lands.
2. Carousel follows 24–48 hrs laterSame story expanded — more depth, more text, room for what didn't fit the video.
3. Carousel references the reel"If you saw the reel, here's the part I couldn't fit." Two formats compound, not compete.

Who owns what

RoleAuthority

Alex / Jason / JustinCan suggest, edit, push back. Cannot override.
If a beat in this brief feels wrong to DawonThe brief is wrong, not her. Edit the brief.
A note on this being her story

The brief talks about Dawon in third person because Alex is writing it. When she films, it's first person — "I was," "I tried," "I found out." Dawon's voice is the only voice the audience hears. Alex, Jason, and Justin appear as people in her story, never as co-narrators.

The core principle — the story doesn't end

Stories have endings. Brands don't. Every episode in this brief is a checkpoint, not a destination. The origin arc has a beginning — Episode 1, the 2022 obsession — but no closing curtain. Episode 15 is not a finale. It's a hand-off from where we came from to what's happening as it happens, and the storytelling continues in the same voice, by the same person, with the same patience.

This matters because every storytelling instinct Dawon has absorbed — from TV, film, school, TikTok — pushes toward arcs that resolve. Brand storytelling resists that. The brand is a living thing. The story is continuous: learning, finding, executing, guiding, becoming better. The audience comes back not for the next finale, but because the journey hasn't stopped.

Apply this rule at the sentence level: no closing language, no "this is the end," no "wrap-up." When an episode finishes, it opens into what's next. When the origin arc finishes airing, the chapter that follows is still the same story — just told from now instead of then.

§ 01 · voice

Dawon's voice — the rules.

She's not a teacher. She's not a guru. She's a person who tried things, broke a few of them, and is sharing what she found.

The posture

Every episode is told from inside the experience, not from above it. The frame is "here's what I tried, and here's what I learned" — never "here's what you should do." The audience is brought along as a witness, not a student.

This is the difference between Glossier's Into the Gloss ("here's what I use") and a beauty influencer's "10 products you need" video. Pheeew sits firmly on the first side of that line.

Sentence-level rules

Do say

  • "Here's what I found out."
  • "In my case, I did X."
  • "I tried Y. It didn't work for me. So I tried Z."
  • "I'm not a doctor. This is just what helped me."
  • "I used to think... and then I learned..."
  • "I don't know if this works for everyone."

Never say

  • "You need to..."
  • "Everyone should..."
  • "The truth is..."
  • "This is the secret..."
  • "It changed my life forever."
  • "The best version of myself."

The discovery cadence

Most episodes follow a four-beat structure: setup (here's where I was), turn (here's what happened or what I tried), aftermath (here's what I noticed or felt), learning (here's what I take from it now). The learning is small and personal — never universal, never prescriptive.

Each beat is a moment, not a paragraph. The reel format forces this discipline. The carousel inherits the same discipline — every slide is one beat, not three.

Language register

Primary language: English. Korean appears in moments where it's authentic — food memories, family contexts, mom's cooking, what a doctor said. Never as a bilingual gimmick or to flex authenticity. If Korean shows up, English subtitles are mandatory on the reel and on the carousel slide.

Profanity: avoided. Even when warranted. The brand has to be safe to share with a relative who watches over your shoulder.

Specificity over generality, always. "I ate one chicken breast and a handful of mushrooms" lands harder than "I undereat." "I tried prune juice, then magnesium, then a doctor-prescribed laxative" lands harder than "I tried everything."

§ 02 · safety

Safety guardrails & the words we don't use.

This is not optional content. These rules apply to every episode, every caption, every comment Dawon replies to.

Read this section before filming any episode

Episodes 1–4 touch eating-disorder-adjacent territory and prescription drug misuse. The framing is non-negotiable. If a take feels wrong to Dawon, Alex, or anyone else reviewing — kill it and re-shoot. We can always make more content. We can't unship harm.

Words we don't use, ever

Don't saySay insteadWhy
Depression"Emotional low times" · "I was struggling emotionally" · "I felt heavy" · "It was hard for me mentally"Clinical diagnosis. Loaded. Reserved for people speaking about a real diagnosis they have.
Eating disorder (as a label for myself)"My relationship with food was broken" · "I wasn't eating well" · "I had a hard time with food"Diagnostic label. Same rule.
I was anorexic / bulimic"I starved myself" · "I restricted what I ate"Naming a clinical condition you weren't formally diagnosed with crosses a line.
Toxic (used as a vibe word)Reserve only for things that were literally toxic — the diet pills, the cosmetic clinic prescribing pattern.Overuse drains its meaning. We use it where it earns its weight.
Cured / fixed me"Helped me" · "Supported me" · "Made a difference"FDA and FTC both care. So does the audience.
"My best self" · "looking my best"If used: in scare quotes, as the irony — "what I thought was my best."Reframing the beauty standard, not endorsing it.
Skinny"Thin" — sparingly. Better: don't describe weight at all.Body-comparison language. Avoid where possible.
Naming specific weight in pounds or kilogramsDon't.Triggering. Invites comparison. Adds nothing to the story.
"Detox" (when describing what Pheeew or anything does)Don't use. Use specific function language instead — "supports digestion," "feeds beneficial gut bacteria."Pseudoscience flag. Regulatory flag.

The disclaimer block

This appears in the caption of every episode that touches food, body, restriction, or medication. Pinned in episodes 1–4 caption. Linked in every other episode caption.

What I share here is my experience — not advice. I'm not a doctor, dietitian, or therapist. If you're struggling with how you feel about food or your body, please reach out to someone qualified to help. I share my story to help people feel less alone, never to suggest what anyone else should do.

The diet pill framing rule

Episode 2 is the highest-risk episode in the entire arc. The way Dawon talks about the prescription pills she took matters enormously. Three rules:

  1. Never name the specific medication. Even if she remembers the name. The risk of someone searching the name and seeking it out is higher than the storytelling benefit.
  2. Frame it as a warning, not a recipe. The episode is structurally about how unregulated the cosmetic-clinic prescribing pattern was, not what the pills did. The villain is the system, not the substance.
  3. The post-pill aftermath is the lesson. The brain fog, the gut shutdown, the emotional cost — these are what the audience walks away with. The high is not romanticized. The "it worked, I lost weight" beat is told as the tragedy, not the triumph.
Disclaimer specific to episode 2

This episode requires an additional disclaimer in the caption and as a slide in the carousel: "The pills I describe were prescription medications used cosmetically through skincare clinics. They are dangerous. I am not naming them and not recommending them. If you've been prescribed something for weight loss that you're unsure about, please consult an actual medical doctor — not a cosmetic clinic."

What never appears on camera

  • Before/after weight loss photos of any kind
  • Scale numbers · body measurements · BMI
  • Mirror selfies focused on body changes
  • Food restriction "what I eat in a day" content
  • The actual pills (no pictures, no demonstrations, no packaging)
  • Other people who are recognizably struggling — only Dawon's own story

Comment moderation rules

Episodes 1–4 will attract three kinds of comments: people sharing their own story (mostly), people asking for medical advice (some), people glorifying the restriction (rare but real). Dawon and Alex review and respond to the first within 24 hours, redirect the second to a doctor with empathy, and either ignore or quietly hide the third. We do not engage with restriction-glorifying comments — engagement amplifies them.

If a comment indicates someone is in active crisis, we DM them privately with the relevant helpline and a short message. Then leave them be. We are not a counseling service. We are a brand that knows when to send someone toward real help.

The emotional beat

The cosmetic clinic prescribing pattern is named — not the pills. The audience learns that the version of "weight loss support" Dawon was offered was prescription-only medication being handed out by skincare clinics for cosmetic reasons. The villain is the system that made this normal, not the substance itself. The aftermath is the lesson — brain fog, gut shutdown, emotional cost.

Pre-shoot checklist for Episode 2

Before filming: (1) the specific medication name is NEVER spoken or shown, (2) the additional disclaimer is in the caption and on a slide, (3) the brain-function impact is hedged as "I felt like" / "I noticed" — never claimed as medical fact, (4) Dawon and Alex both review the final cut before posting. Two-person sign-off, mandatory.

§ 04 · production

Visual + production rules.

The look has to feel like Dawon. Not a brand video. Not an influencer. A person who decided to share what happened.

Visual mood — what we are

  • Warm cream-and-brown palette matched to the Pheeew brand accent (#8B4A2C). Soft, ivory, washed lighting. Natural light wherever possible.
  • Handheld, not stabilized. Slight motion is honest. A perfectly still shot reads as agency. A small wobble reads as a real person.
  • Real spaces. Dawon's kitchen, her desk, her walk, her coffee shop. Not a studio. Not a sponsored background. The Pheeew product when it shows up looks at home in a real life.
  • Negative space. Generous margins. Long exhales between cuts. Don't fill every second of the reel.

Visual anti-mood — what we are not

  • No gym selfie aesthetic. No mirror shots focused on the body
  • No "what I eat in a day" framing
  • No bright/cold studio lighting
  • No on-screen text that screams (max 2 lines per text overlay, max 60% screen coverage)
  • No before/after photos of anything personal — body, face, hair, skin, weight
  • No influencer cadence (fast cuts, trending sounds layered over original audio, "Y'all..." openings)

Equipment baseline

Everything in the origin arc can be filmed on an iPhone 14 Pro or newer. No external rigs required. Optional adds: a small clip-on lavalier mic ($30) for clean voice audio, a phone tripod ($20) for static shots. Total kit budget under $100. This setup carries forward into the ongoing chapter too — same equipment, same look, same person.

§ 05 · cadence

The continuous timeline.

The origin arc runs across the first 8 weeks. From week 9 onward, the storytelling continues in the same voice — only the timeframe shifts from then to now. There is no break between them. The 12-week view below is a starting horizon, not a closed plan.

This is one timeline, not two phases

The brief originally split this section into "Phase 1" and "Phase 2." That framing was wrong. The origin arc and what follows are the same story, told by the same person, with the same rhythm. The only thing that changes around week 9 is the verb tense — past becomes present. Dawon does not "wrap" anything. The next post is just the next post.

Posting frequency rules

  • Never miss a posted-day. If Dawon misses Tuesday, she posts Wednesday and shifts the rest of the week. Consistency builds the audience; missed days destroy it.
  • One post per day, max. Don't double-post. The audience needs space to process each one.

§ 07 · the graduation plan

How Dawon steps back as the brand evolves.

We start with Dawon at the center because the brand needs a person to belong to. We plan to ease her back from daily visibility because the brand needs to be bigger than any one person before it scales. The evolution is continuous — not staged.

This section is for the four of us. It's not in Dawon's working script. It's the strategic horizon — what we're building toward, so every decision today is also a decision about where the brand stands in three years. The brand never finishes evolving; this plan describes the direction, not a destination.

There are three modes, not three seasons

The brand operates in three modes — Dawon-led, distributed voices, and brand-as-character. All three exist at every moment. What changes over time is the proportion of each. Early on, the mix is heavy on Dawon-led. Later, the mix shifts toward brand-as-character. There is no clean cutover between them — no "now we're in Mode 2." The dial just moves.

Mode 1 · Dawon-led

  • Dawon is the only voice on Pheeew's @saypheeew channels
  • Alex, Jason, Justin do not appear on Pheeew's owned media (occasional fleeting appearance allowed; no co-narration)
  • External press requests are routed to Dawon first; Alex backstops for strategy-heavy interviews (where founder/CEO voice is what's being asked for)
  • Goal: build a recognizable brand voice that's emotionally identifiable as Dawon's, with Pheeew quietly underneath
  • Dominant in the first ~3 months. Still present (just smaller) for years afterward

Mode 2 · Distributed voices

  • Customer voices enter — real Pheeew drinkers share their gut journey, often in their own reels we re-share with permission
  • Alex emerges as the visible founder/CEO in business-press contexts (podcast appearances, investor mentor interviews, B2B retail buyer conversations) — but stays off the @saypheeew feed
  • Jason and Justin appear in formal team intros (one-pager bios, "meet the founders" carousel — non-narrative, formal) — they don't become content characters
  • Dawon's content shifts gradually from origin storytelling to live documentation + reviews + behind-the-scenes — less "this is who I am," more "this is what we're doing"
  • Goal: distribute the brand voice so it isn't single-points-of-failure on Dawon
  • Starts emerging around month 4–6. Becomes the dominant mode by month 9–12

Mode 3 · Brand-as-character

  • The brand develops its own voice that isn't anchored to any single founder
  • Dawon's appearances become editorial moments (campaign launches, anniversary moments, occasional founder essays) — not weekly
  • UGC and customer-shot content becomes the majority of feed content
  • Pheeew speaks more as a category POV (gut health, clean wellness, synbiotic literacy) — less as one person's biography
  • Goal: Pheeew is identifiable to the audience without Dawon needing to be in the frame. The brand has earned the right to outlast its founders' faces
  • Starts being meaningful around month 10–12. Becomes dominant by month 18+. Continues to deepen for as long as the brand exists

What we never give up, even when the mix tilts fully toward Mode 3

  • The origin arc stays in the archive. It is the brand's beginning and customers will keep finding it. We do not take any of it down
  • Dawon stays on @saypheeew as a credited author of the brand's voice. Easing her back from being the daily face is not the same as removing her from the brand
  • The honesty about gut health + body relationships + the wellness industry stays — that's the bone structure. Whoever speaks for the brand carries that posture

Signals that the mix should shift

These are signals — not switches. When they show up, we adjust the proportion. We don't flip a mode.

  • Lean more on distributed voices (Mode 2) when: we've shipped to ~10K customers or reached ~100K Instagram followers, whichever comes first. Both signal there's enough audience and enough customer story to absorb new voices without diluting Dawon's
  • Lean more on brand-as-character (Mode 3) when: we hit ~$1M monthly revenue run rate or the first retail door (Whole Foods, Sprouts, Erewhon). Either is the moment the brand needs to stand independent enough to scale beyond a single face
  • Pull-the-cord scenarios: if Dawon ever wants out of the public-facing role earlier than these signals would suggest, we accelerate. The plan respects her. She is not contractually bound to her own face
  • Snap-back scenarios: if a launch moment, anniversary, crisis, or new chapter warrants Dawon's voice back at the center, we tilt the mix back temporarily. The dial moves both ways
A note to Dawon, from Alex

You're not signing up to be Pheeew's face forever. You're signing up to start the brand off in a way only you can. We've talked about this — the plan is to ease you back when the time is right, and to do that with intention, not because anyone got tired. The work you do at the beginning is the part of Pheeew that lasts the longest, even after your face stops being on every post. There's no end to the story — only changes in who's telling it on which days. You author the voice; whoever speaks for the brand later, speaks in it.
