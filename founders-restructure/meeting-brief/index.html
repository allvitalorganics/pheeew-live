Chapter 1

Why this restructure.

What changed since April

Pheeew was incorporated in March 2026 with Alex (CEO). In April 2026, Jason (COO) and Dawon (CBO/CDO) joined as co-founders, splitting equity roughly 31.5% / 29.7% / 28.8% (with 10% reserved pool).

Justin (Alex's brother) has offered to join as the fourth co-founder. He brings:

  1. $30,000 in capital (more than 4× what each original founder contributed)
  2. VC network access through mentor relationships including a VC partner and the CEO of ABIB Cosmetic (who handles his own angel investments)
  3. Front-of-house role as Chief Growth Officer (CGO) — partnerships, creator/influencer sourcing, retail buyer outreach, investor sourcing, marketing campaigns, biz dev

Why the structure has to change

Adding a fourth co-founder to a near-equal 3-founder split would create a 4-way equal split (~24/23/22/21). VCs reading this cap table during diligence often see four co-founders with similar % as a governance risk: "Who actually decides? Who's the captain?"

Justin's mentors flagged this as a fundability concern. The cleanest fix is to concentrate equity toward Alex as the lead founder, while keeping the other founders meaningfully invested. This is a common pattern for founder-led brand companies (Facebook, Snap, Stripe).

💡
In plain English When VCs look at a startup with 4 equal co-founders, they get nervous about who's actually in charge. If one person has clearly the most equity, the story is simpler: "Alex is the founder; the others are co-builders." This is better for raising money later.

Chapter 5

The gift mechanism — why it's tax-clean.

The key innovation: Justin and Jason each give Alex a personal cash gift, and Alex uses that money to buy his refresh shares. This is a real and legal mechanism.

IRS rules (2026)

  • Annual gift tax exclusion: $19,000 per recipient. Anyone can gift up to $19,000 to any one person per year with no tax, no form filing, no record-keeping requirements.
  • Both gifts ($9,458 each) are well under $19,000 — no Form 709, no IRS reporting, no tax owed.
  • Gifts are not taxable income to the recipient. Alex doesn't owe income tax on the $18,916 he receives.
  • Justin and Jason don't get a tax deduction (gifts aren't deductible to giver), but they don't owe gift tax either.

Why this works legally

This is not a structure being invented for tax avoidance. Personal gifts between siblings (Justin → Alex) and close friends (Jason → Alex) for the purpose of investing in a closely-held business are common and well-documented in corporate finance practice. The annual exclusion is specifically designed for transfers like this.

What we document

To make this airtight, two short Gift Letters will be drafted:

  1. Letter from Justin to Alex — "I am gifting $9,458 to my brother Seungjoon Kim, with no expectation of repayment or return"
  2. Letter from Jason to Alex — Same template

Each letter is one page, signed by the giver. Total drafting time: 10 minutes per letter.

💡
In plain English Family and friends can gift each other up to $19,000/year with zero tax paperwork. Justin and Jason each gift Alex less than $19K, so it's totally clean. Alex uses that gifted money to buy more of his own company stock. The whole thing is one step legal, no forms, no fees.

Chapter 7

Voting & governance — how Alex stays in control.

Alex's 40% equity is significant, but doesn't give him outright voting majority (>50%) on stockholder matters. The structure addresses this through layered governance, not equity concentration alone.

Layer 1: Sole Director (already in place)

Alex is the Sole Director of Pheeew, Inc. The Board controls operational decisions (officer hiring/firing, budgets, contracts, strategic plans). Until the board expands (typically at Series A), Alex has complete operational authority regardless of stockholder votes.

Layer 2: Voting Agreement (new, today)

Jason, Justin, and Dawon will grant Alex an irrevocable proxy on stockholder votes until the earlier of:

  • Series A round closing, OR
  • 5 years from the agreement date

This gives Alex 90% voting power on stockholder matters (charter amendments, sale of company, IPO, dissolution) for the duration.

Layer 3: Class B Super-Voting Stock (planned for Series A)

When Pheeew raises Series A, Class B Common Stock with 10:1 voting power will be issued to Alex as part of the round terms. This gives Alex ~70%+ voting power permanently through future dilution rounds. Standard founder-control mechanism used by Google, Facebook, Snap, Stripe, Coinbase, etc.

💡
In plain English Alex's 40% isn't a "vote majority" by itself. But three layers of governance give him real control: (1) he's the Sole Director (runs daily operations), (2) the other three founders give him their voting power on big company-wide decisions until Series A or 5 years, (3) at Series A we'll structure special voting stock for Alex that locks in his control permanently. Standard practice for founder-led companies.
