Chapter 5

The gift mechanism — why it's tax-clean.

The key innovation: Justin and Jason each give Alex a personal cash gift, and Alex uses that money to buy his refresh shares. This is a real and legal mechanism.

IRS rules (2026)

  • Annual gift tax exclusion: $19,000 per recipient. Anyone can gift up to $19,000 to any one person per year with no tax, no form filing, no record-keeping requirements.
  • Both gifts ($9,458 each) are well under $19,000 — no Form 709, no IRS reporting, no tax owed.
  • Gifts are not taxable income to the recipient. Alex doesn't owe income tax on the $18,916 he receives.
  • Justin and Jason don't get a tax deduction (gifts aren't deductible to giver), but they don't owe gift tax either.

Why this works legally

This is not a structure being invented for tax avoidance. Personal gifts between siblings (Justin → Alex) and close friends (Jason → Alex) for the purpose of investing in a closely-held business are common and well-documented in corporate finance practice. The annual exclusion is specifically designed for transfers like this.

What we document

To make this airtight, two short Gift Letters will be drafted:

  1. Letter from Justin to Alex — "I am gifting $9,458 to my brother Seungjoon Kim, with no expectation of repayment or return"
  2. Letter from Jason to Alex — Same template

Each letter is one page, signed by the giver. Total drafting time: 10 minutes per letter.

💡
In plain English Family and friends can gift each other up to $19,000/year with zero tax paperwork. Justin and Jason each gift Alex less than $19K, so it's totally clean. Alex uses that gifted money to buy more of his own company stock. The whole thing is one step legal, no forms, no fees.

Foundational questions & answers.

Q1. Why stock OPTIONS instead of direct EQUITY for the KPI grants?

Stock options and restricted stock are different instruments with different mechanics. For performance-based founder grants, options are preferred because:

  1. Performance-conditioned vesting: Options can have vesting tied to BOTH a service requirement AND a performance trigger. Direct equity grants typically only allow time-based vesting — there's no clean way to say "you only get this stock if you hit $50K revenue."
  2. Clean forfeiture: If the KPI isn't met, the option simply expires — no tax owed, no paperwork mess. With direct equity grants, the company would have to draft repurchase rights and execute share repurchases manually.
  3. Tax efficiency: Options have no taxable event at grant. Direct equity grants require an 83(b) filing within 30 days and create a tax timing problem if linked to performance.
  4. Industry standard: Performance-conditioned options are the standard tool. VCs read them without confusion.
  5. No upfront cash requirement: Recipient doesn't pay anything at grant — only pays the strike price when exercising (after vesting).
💡
In plain English Options are like a coupon that says "you can buy this stock at this cheap price once you've earned it." Direct equity is like getting the stock right now. Options are cleaner for performance-based awards because if you don't hit the milestone, the coupon just expires — no taxes, no paperwork. It's the standard startup tool for this exact situation.

Q2. Where do the stock options come from? Do we issue more shares?

Options come from the existing 10% equity pool that's already in the cap table. We do NOT issue additional shares.

The 10% pool (2,880,000 shares of the 28,800,000 fully diluted) sits as "authorized but unissued" — the shares exist on the company's books as reserved, but no one owns them yet. When KPI options vest and are exercised:

  1. The recipient (Jason, Justin, or Dawon) pays the strike price ($0.00226/share)
  2. The shares move from the pool's "reserved" status to the recipient's "owned" status
  3. The pool shrinks by that number of shares
  4. The recipient's % of company grows
  5. Other founders' percentages don't change (because the shares were already counted in the fully diluted total)
💡
In plain English The pool is like a stash of pre-allocated slices waiting to be assigned. When KPIs hit, we're not making a new pie — we're just moving slices from the "reserved" bucket to "Jason owns it" bucket. Other founders' slices don't get smaller because those reserved slices were already counted in the total.

Pheeew formally incorporated March 24, 2026 with Alex as sole founder/CEO. He's been carrying the longest founder risk — financial, legal, reputational, opportunity cost. Jason joined in April; Justin joins now in May.
  2. CEO operational burden: Going forward — fundraising, strategic vision, founder relationships, vendor negotiations, financial accountability.
  3. VC fundability: Justin's VC mentors flagged that 4-way equal splits are red flags. Clear founder majority makes Pheeew more fundable.
  4. Decision velocity: One clear leader can move fast. Consensus governance among 4 equals is slower.
  5. Dilution buffer: Alex's larger starting position lets him sustain meaningful equity through 3+ dilution rounds.

Q4. Why use the gift mechanism for Alex's refresh — why not just give Alex shares as compensation?

Path A (current proposal) — Gift mechanism. Justin and Jason each gift Alex a small amount (under $19,000 each, under the federal annual gift exclusion). Alex uses the gifted money to buy refresh shares from Pheeew at FMV ($0.00226/share).

  • Tax on Alex: $0 (gifts aren't income to recipient)
  • Tax on Justin: $0 (under $19K exclusion, no Form 709)
  • Tax on Jason: $0 (under $19K exclusion, no Form 709)
  • Paperwork: 1-2 gift letters, 10 minutes each, no notary, no IRS filing
  • Risk: very low (well-established legal mechanism)

Path B — Compensation mechanism. Alex receives refresh shares directly from Pheeew as compensation for services rendered. He pays nothing (or par value).

  • Tax on Alex: ~$5,500 – $8,000 of personal income tax (the spread between FMV $0.00226 and what he paid is treated as ordinary compensation income, $18,916 of taxable income at his marginal rate)
  • Tax on Justin/Jason: $0
  • Paperwork: Pheeew needs to issue Alex a W-2 showing the compensation income; payroll setup required
  • Risk: medium (409A audit risk if IRS challenges the FMV); company books a comp expense it can't currently use as deduction (pre-revenue)

Path C — Alex pays his own cash. Alex pulls $18,916 from his personal account and pays Pheeew directly for the refresh shares.

  • Tax on Alex: $0
  • Paperwork: minimal
  • Catch: Alex doesn't want to put in more cash (locked principle)

Q5. How do stocks get "registered" or proven? How does anyone know our stock price? How does paper become money? Can we adjust as we like internally?

Great fundamental question. Let me address each part.

Where stocks are "registered" for a private company like Pheeew:

  1. Internal stock ledger — the authoritative record. Pheeew maintains this in Pheeew_Master_Corporate_Records/04_Stock_Certificates/ and the Cap Table v2. Alex as Secretary signs off on every issuance. Single source of truth.
  2. State of incorporation — Delaware has Pheeew in their corporate registry, but only knows the total authorized shares (currently 10M, becoming 30M with the Cert of Amendment). Delaware doesn't track who owns what.
  3. IRS — has Pheeew's EIN; receives annual tax returns (Form 1120) showing capital structure changes.
  4. Stripe Atlas dashboard — administrative platform, doesn't automatically track ongoing equity transactions. We update our own records.
  5. Mercury Bank — knows about capital contributions (sees wires) but not about share ownership.

There is no public "stock price" for Pheeew. There won't be one until a liquidity event.

Why is this different from public companies?

Public companies file with the SEC (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K) and trade on stock exchanges. Their prices update second-to-second based on real-time trading.

Private companies have no continuously traded price. The "value per share" exists only at transaction moments:

  • Founder grants → FMV at grant date (set by the company, audit-defensible)
  • Investor purchases → price negotiated at funding rounds
  • Secondary sales → price negotiated between private parties

Between these transactions, there's no "price" because no one is buying or selling.

Why would someone buy our stock?

  1. Belief in growth + future liquidity event. Angel investors, VCs, strategic investors buy expecting an acquisition, IPO, or secondary sale in 3-7 years at a much higher valuation.
  2. Strategic value. Larger companies sometimes invest for partnership reasons (technology access, market entry, cross-selling).
  3. Founder/employee upside. People accept stock as part of compensation, betting on company's future value.

No one is buying Pheeew stock today expecting to flip it next week. They're investing in 3-7 year growth.

How does paper (equity) become money? Three paths:

  1. Acquisition (M&A) — Most common exit for early-stage companies. A larger company (PepsiCo, Unilever, Nestlé, PE firm) buys Pheeew. Shareholders receive cash or acquirer's stock. Examples in our category: Poppi sold to PepsiCo for $1.95B in 2025; Bare Foods to PepsiCo for $200M+.
  2. IPO (Initial Public Offering) — Pheeew lists on a stock exchange (NYSE, NASDAQ). Shares become publicly tradeable. Less common for early-stage CPG — typically 7-10+ years out.
  3. Secondary sales — Founders/early employees sell some shares to new investors at later funding rounds. Common at Series B/C+. Lets early holders get partial liquidity without full exit.

Without one of these events, founder shares are essentially paper. They have legal value, vote rights, dividend rights (if any) — but they can't be converted to cash through normal means.

Can we adjust the cap table "as we like" internally?

Yes — within real rules. Adjustments must:

  1. Be approved by appropriate parties (Board, stockholders) depending on the action
  2. Comply with Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL)
  3. Have proper "consideration" (you can't just hand out shares; there must be cash, services, or value exchange)
  4. Stay within the authorized share count from the Certificate of Incorporation
  5. Be properly documented in corporate records
  6. Be reportable on annual tax filings (IRS Form 1120)
  7. Not create undisclosed tax events for recipients

When external parties look at us, they see the FULL record. Investors during diligence, auditors during financings, acquirers during M&A — they all review the cap table from inception. If we did anything undocumented or improper, it surfaces. Cap table integrity is critical for raising capital.

So while we have flexibility, we operate under real rules. The cap table IS the company. We can't doctor it without consequences — but we CAN structure new transactions properly to achieve specific economic outcomes.

💡
In plain English Pheeew's stock exists only on internal records (kept by Alex as Secretary) plus Delaware's corporate registry (which only shows total authorized shares, not who owns what). There's no public "stock price" until we either: (1) get acquired by another company, (2) go public on a stock exchange, or (3) have someone buy shares from existing stockholders at a future round. Until one of those happens, our shares are "paper" — legally real, but not cash. We can adjust the cap table internally, but only with proper approval, real consideration, and documentation. Future investors, acquirers, and the IRS all eventually see the full record.

Q9. How do stock options actually work? Terms, exercise, vesting, cliff?

Grant date: When the option is awarded. Recipient owns the OPTION (right to buy), not the stock yet.

Strike price: The price at which you can buy shares once vested. Set at FMV at grant ($0.00226/share for Pheeew). You only profit if stock appreciates above the strike.

Vesting — Pheeew's KPI options use DOUBLE-TRIGGER:

  1. Performance trigger — the specific KPI must be achieved
  2. Service trigger — 12 months of continuous service AFTER the performance trigger is met

BOTH conditions must occur. If you leave before either, the option is forfeited (returns to pool).

Compare to founder direct equity (RSPA shares — what you already have):

Founder Shares (RSPA)KPI Options
Acquisition methodBought at grant for cashEarned through performance + service
Vesting typeTime-based onlyDouble-trigger (performance + service)
Vesting schedule4-year, 1-year cliffUntil KPI hits + 12mo after
What if you leave early?Unvested shares repurchasedUnvested options return to pool
83(b) election needed?Yes (within 30 days)No (options aren't restricted property at grant)

Yes, the two vesting schedules are separate.

Exercise: Once vested, pay the strike price and the option becomes actual stock.

Expiration: Options typically expire 10 years after grant OR 90 days after termination (whichever sooner).

Tax treatment at exercise: ISO route — no regular income tax at exercise, AMT may apply on spread; long-term capital gains if held long enough. NSO route — ordinary income tax on the spread. We structure as ISOs where eligible.

💡
In plain English Options work like this — you receive a coupon that says "you can buy [number] shares at $0.00226 each once you've earned it." Two things must happen: (1) you hit your KPI milestone, and (2) you stay with the company for 12 months after hitting it. Once both happen, you pay the strike price and the coupon becomes actual stock. You have 10 years to use the coupon, or 90 days after leaving. Your founder shares vest on a separate, simpler schedule (4 years total, 25% at the one-year cliff, monthly after). Two separate vesting clocks.
