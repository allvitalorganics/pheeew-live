Mock · full post
Your gut bacteria make stress-regulating chemicals. The recipe is fiber.
Researchers at the Medical University of Vienna (Neurobiology of Stress, May 16) measured cortisol reactivity in adults with varying gut microbiome diversity. People with more diverse microbiomes showed measurably calmer cortisol responses to acute stress tests. The mechanism: short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) — acetate, butyrate, propionate — produced when gut bacteria ferment fiber. Those metabolites cross into the bloodstream and modulate the brain's stress signaling.
So the bacteria that make you calmer under stress are the same bacteria that need fiber to live. Skip the fiber, the bacteria starve, the SCFAs stop showing up. Stress-eating is probably way less about willpower than the biology suggests.
What's your fiber count today? Honest answers only.

Mock · full post
Neuroscientists keep finding the same answer in the gut.
A new study from University of Technology Sydney and Mass General/Harvard (reported May 16) used AI trained on multi-modal patient data, including microbiome composition, to identify consistent gut bacteria patterns in Alzheimer's patients across n≈10,000 records. Compared to age-matched controls, Alzheimer's patients showed measurably lower microbial diversity and specific shifts in inflammation-related bacterial populations.
This doesn't mean fiber prevents Alzheimer's. It means science keeps mapping the gut-brain connection in higher and higher resolution. The "how do I take care of my brain" question increasingly has "how do I take care of my gut" as one of its long-term answers. The everyday version of that takeaway is unglamorous: diverse plants, more fiber, less sugar, fewer all-or-nothing diet swings.
The picture keeps getting bigger.
